# Safety notes — 3-Tier Hexagonal Walnut Lazy Susan

Build-specific safety, not generic shop policy. The two real hazards on
this build are **bandsaw cuts on the hex perimeter** and **finishing-rag
spontaneous combustion**. Everything else is routine.

## PPE for this build

- Safety glasses: every power-tool step (bandsaw, drill press, router,
  orbital sander).
- Hearing protection: bandsaw and router. The router especially — a
  handheld router on hardwood is in the 90+ dB range.
- Dust mask (N95 minimum): walnut dust is a known sensitizer and respiratory
  irritant. Wear it for sanding and for the bandsaw resawing of glue-up
  stock.
- No gloves at the bandsaw or drill press. Gloves can catch in rotating
  parts and pull a hand into the blade. Bare hands or close-fitting work
  gloves only when handling stock between cuts.
- Long sleeves rolled up; loose clothing and long hair tied back at every
  power tool.

## Tool-specific risks

- **Bandsaw — the highest-risk tool on this build.** Six perimeter cuts per
  tier, 18 cuts total, all in 3/4" walnut.
  - Set blade guides 1/4" above stock. No higher.
  - Push stick or push block in the off-hand at all times when feeding
    near the blade.
  - Never reach around the blade to clear an offcut. Stop the saw, wait
    for the blade to stop, then clear.
  - If a cut starts wandering off the line, **don't twist the stock**.
    Back the stock out (with the saw running, gently) and re-enter the
    cut.
  - Sharp blade only. The 1/2" 3-TPI blade in the BOM is sized for this
    work; a dull blade is the leading cause of bandsaw wandering and
    burns and forces the maker to push harder, which is when injuries
    happen.
- **Drill press — low risk if stock is clamped.** The bearing-mount holes
  are small (1/16" pilots, 1/2" thru-hole) but the 1/2" hole in 3/4"
  walnut wants to grab the bit on breakthrough. **Clamp the workpiece**;
  don't hand-hold.
- **Handheld router — climb-cut and grain tear-out.** Always feed the
  router so the bit's rotation pulls the router *into* the work, not
  away. On the top face going clockwise around the hex perimeter is
  correct; counter-clockwise is a climb cut and the router will run away.
  Light passes; don't try to take a 1/4" roundover in one hit if the wood
  fights back — drop to 1/8" first, then 1/4".
- **Orbital sander — vibration and dust.** Two-handed grip. Empty the
  collection bag often. Walnut dust on the floor is slippery.

## Material-specific risks

- **Walnut dust is a sensitizer.** Repeated unprotected exposure causes
  respiratory irritation in some people and allergic reactions in others.
  Mask up for sanding. Vacuum (don't sweep) at the end of the day.
- **Walnut is hard on hand tools.** The hand-plane work on hex sides
  (Step 5) wants a sharp blade — touch up the iron between tiers if it
  starts tearing instead of slicing.
- **Glue-up squeeze-out** on walnut leaves white residue under finish if
  not wiped fully wet, then sanded out. Wipe within 5 minutes of clamping.

## Process-specific risks

- **Hexagon vertices on the bandsaw.** As you finish each cut, the offcut
  separates and can fall toward the blade. Have the off-hand on a push
  block, not on the offcut. Slow the feed rate over the last inch.
- **Routing the rounded edge at the vertices.** The router bit's bearing
  rides the 120° corner; the bit wants to dig in at that geometry.
  **Slow down through every vertex** and lift the router cleanly off the
  end of each side rather than trying to round the corner with the bit
  engaged.
- **Pinch points during glue-up.** F-clamps under tension can release
  energy if a slip happens. Stage the clamps before opening glue.

## Emergency / fire / fume notes

- **Oil-soaked rags spontaneously combust.** Danish oil and most wipe-on
  finishes generate heat as they cure. A wadded oily rag in a trash can
  has started shop fires. **After every finishing session, lay rags
  flat on concrete or in a sealed metal can with water until fully
  dry, then dispose.** This is the single highest-fire-risk step on the
  whole build.
- **Walnut dust + ignition source = nuisance flash.** Not a deflagration
  hazard at home-shop scale, but don't sand near an open flame and empty
  the sander bag away from the shop.
- **First aid kit** within reach of the bandsaw. Have a phone where you
  can grab it left-handed if your right hand is the one in trouble.
- **No solo bandsaw work after 10 PM tired.** Rule of thumb that catches
  more accidents than any other policy.
