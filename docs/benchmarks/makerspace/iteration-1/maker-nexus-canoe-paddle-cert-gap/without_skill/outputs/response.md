# Steam-Bent Ash Canoe Paddle at Maker Nexus — Figuring Out Your Path

Fun project. Ash is a great choice — it steam-bends beautifully, has a long history in paddle and canoe construction, and is forgiving for a first carved paddle. Here's how I'd think about your situation.

> **Caveat up front:** I don't have specific knowledge of Maker Nexus's current class schedule, certification curriculum, or shop rules. Anything I say about "their bandsaw class" or "their policies" is a reasonable guess based on how most makerspaces operate. Confirm everything with a shop steward or the class calendar before you commit.

---

## The honest answer: yes, you almost certainly want the bandsaw cert

For a steam-bent ash paddle, the bandsaw isn't strictly required — but skipping it makes the project meaningfully harder and probably less safe than the alternatives. Here's why it keeps coming up:

**Where the bandsaw earns its keep on a paddle build:**
1. **Rough-cutting the blank to the paddle profile** (the side-view silhouette: grip, shaft, blade outline). This is the single biggest time-saver and the cleanest way to get curves into a 5–6 ft plank.
2. **Resawing** if you're laminating (e.g., splitting a thicker board into two book-matched halves around a bent shaft, or adding a contrasting wood for the grip/tip).
3. **Trimming the bent shaft** after steam-bending and drying — bent stock is awkward on most other tools.

**What you could substitute** (woodshop-only path):
- A jigsaw or coping saw for the profile cut. Doable, slower, harder to keep square on 8/4 ash.
- A bowsaw or hand saw for rough shaping. Traditional and very satisfying, but adds hours.
- Drawknife, spokeshave, and block plane do most of the *real* shaping anyway — those don't need a bandsaw cert.

So the realistic options are:

### Option A — Take the bandsaw class first, then build (recommended)
Most makerspaces run bandsaw certs frequently (monthly or more), and they're usually short — often a single 2–3 hour session. If Maker Nexus follows that pattern, you'd lose maybe a week of calendar time and gain a tool you'll use on every future project. For a summer build window, this is almost always the right call.

**Action:** Check Maker Nexus's class calendar for the next bandsaw / "intro to bandsaw" / "stationary saws" certification. Sign up.

### Option B — Build paddle-only with what you're already cleared for
Possible if their woodshop cert covers a jigsaw, drill press, sanders, and hand tools. You'd:
- Mill the blank to thickness on the planer (if you're cleared for it).
- Cut the profile with a jigsaw or by hand.
- Do all shaping with drawknife / spokeshave / rasps / cabinet scrapers / random orbit sander.
- Steam-bend the shaft using a shop-built or borrowed steam box.

This is genuinely how paddles were made for centuries. It just takes longer.

### Option C — Ask if a steward can do the bandsaw cuts for you
Some makerspaces will let a certified member or staff make a one-time cut for an uncertified member, especially for a single rough profile cut. Worth asking, but don't count on it as the plan.

## Other things worth checking at Maker Nexus before you start

- **Steam box:** Do they have one, or will you build/bring your own? A paddle shaft only needs a ~5 ft box, which is easy to build from foam insulation board, plywood, or PVC + a wallpaper steamer or kettle.
- **Steam-bending form / clamping space:** You'll need a bending form (a curved jig) and somewhere to leave the bent shaft clamped for 24–48 hrs to dry in shape. Confirm they're okay with you parking a jig on a bench overnight.
- **Stock sourcing:** Ash has gotten harder to find in some regions because of emerald ash borer. Local hardwood dealers usually still carry it — you want straight-grained, riftsawn or quartersawn 8/4 (about 1.75–2 in thick after milling), at least 5.5–6 ft long, knot-free in the shaft area. Air-dried is better than kiln-dried for steam-bending; KD ash bends fine but needs longer steam time and is slightly more prone to cracking.
- **Finish:** Most shops have ventilation rules about oil finishes (tung, linseed) — easy, but check before you bring a can.

## Suggested path

1. **This week:** Ask the front desk or check the calendar for the next bandsaw certification. Sign up.
2. **While you wait:** Source your ash blank (one good 8/4 board, ~6 ft, will yield one paddle with room to spare). Decide on a paddle style — beavertail, ottertail, voyageur, or a flat-bladed touring paddle. Northwoods Canoe Co., Murat Brierre, Graham Warren, and Bjarne Dahl all have good plans/dimensions online. Sketch a full-size template on butcher paper.
3. **Also while you wait:** Build or locate a steam box and a bending form. The form is the key piece — it sets the shaft's slight curve (most paddles are straight-shafted, but bent-shaft paddles have ~12 degrees at the throat).
4. **Once certified:** Mill, profile-cut on the bandsaw, steam-bend the shaft (if doing a bent-shaft), let it set, then spend the bulk of your time hand-shaping the blade and grip.
5. **Finish:** Multiple thin coats of tung oil or a marine oil/varnish blend. Whip the tip with epoxy or a brass cap if you'll hit rocks.

## A few questions to bring to a shop steward

- "Is there a bandsaw cert I can take in the next 2–3 weeks?"
- "Can I leave a steam-bending jig clamped on a bench overnight?"
- "Any restrictions on bringing in green or wet stock for steaming?"
- "Is there a member who's done a paddle here before I could talk to?" (Makerspaces almost always have someone — and they'll save you hours.)

Good luck. A hand-shaped ash paddle is one of those projects that punches way above its complexity in terms of how good it feels to use the thing afterward.
