# Sourcing notes

## Plywood - the most important call

Baltic birch quality varies wildly by source. Things to look for:

- **Ply count.** Genuine 1/4" Baltic birch is **5-ply**. If you count
  3 plies, it's not Baltic - it's likely Russian birch (no longer
  imported to the US in any volume) or a domestic clone.
- **Voids.** Cut the edge with a sharp knife. There should be no
  visible voids in any of the inner plies. Voids ruin V-carve depth.
- **Face grade.** B/BB means one near-perfect face and one with small
  patches. BB/BB has patches on both. For a sign, B/BB is plenty -
  show the B side.
- **Country of origin.** Russian Baltic birch is effectively
  unavailable in the US since 2022. Most "Baltic birch" sold in 2024-
  2026 is actually Latvian or Finnish - quality is comparable but
  prices are higher than they used to be.

## Bits

The two bits I called out are reusable across many signs. If you're
new to V-carving and don't already own a 60 deg V-bit, this build is a
great excuse to buy one nice one rather than a cheap set.

- Avoid Amazon "carbide V-bits" under $10. Many are lower-grade
  carbide ground at sloppy angles - the actual included angle can be
  off by 5-10 degrees, which changes letter width.
- Amana, Whiteside, and IDC Woodcraft are all reliable.

## Finish

Osmo Polyx-Oil is over-recommended for a reason. The smaller 0.125 L
can is the right size for this scale of project; you can finish dozens
of small signs from one can if you don't leave the lid off.

If you can't find Osmo locally, **Tried & True Original** (boiled
linseed oil + beeswax, no metallic dryers) is a slower-curing but very
similar finish and is often easier to find at independent woodworking
shops.

## What to *not* spend money on

- Pre-cut "sign blank" Baltic birch from craft sites. Marked up 3-5x
  for a square cut you can do in 30 seconds on the table saw.
- "Sign-making bit kits." You only need two bits.
- A laser engraver - explicitly out of scope for this build.
