# Walking-Foot Industrial Machine — Certification Path at Maker Nexus

## Why you need this certification
Maker Nexus separates sewing equipment into three cert tiers:
1. **Domestic sewing machine cert** — covers the Janome/Bernina/Brother machines in the textile area. You have this.
2. **Walking-foot / industrial straight-stitch cert** — covers the Juki DU-1181N (or similar) and the industrial walking-foot. **This is what you need for waxed canvas.**
3. **Specialty cert** — coverstitch, leather post-bed, embroidery (separate path).

A domestic machine CAN sew 12-oz waxed canvas in single layers, but it will struggle or fail at:
- Bound-seam intersections (4-6 layers of waxed canvas + bias)
- Strap anchor points (canvas + foam + webbing + canvas wrap)
- Zipper-tape junctions through facing + body

The walking-foot has synchronized upper and lower feed (the foot itself walks with the feed dogs), preventing layer shift and powering through stacks the domestic foot would skip on.

## Prerequisites (you already have)
- Active Maker Nexus membership in good standing
- Domestic sewing machine cert (logged in your member profile)
- Have completed at least one project on a domestic machine (self-attest)

## The three-step certification

### Step 1: Walking-Foot Orientation class
- **Length:** 90 minutes
- **Frequency:** Most weeks; check the Maker Nexus events calendar. Often Tuesday evenings or Saturday mornings.
- **Cost:** Usually free for members; some specialty classes have a $10-25 materials fee.
- **What's covered:**
  - Threading the industrial (different path from domestic)
  - Bobbin winding and bobbin case (drop-in vs. front-load — industrials are typically front-load)
  - Tension adjustment (top and bobbin)
  - Needle change and needle selection (industrial system 135x17 vs. domestic 130/705H)
  - Foot pressure adjustment for material thickness
  - Stitch length adjustment (no zigzag — these are straight-stitch only)
  - Reverse lever (it's on the front, not a button)
  - Knee lift (huge productivity feature you don't have on a domestic)
  - Servo motor speed control — these run FAST out of the box; the steward will show you how to dial back the max RPM
  - Emergency stop, safety guards, and what to do if thread bird-nests under the throat plate

### Step 2: Supervised first-use sign-off
- **Length:** 30 minutes
- **Cost:** Free, walk-in during staffed textile hours
- **Bring:** A scrap stack of YOUR project material — at least 8 in x 8 in of doubled 12-oz waxed canvas, plus a strip of cotton webbing and bias tape
- **You demonstrate:**
  - Threading the machine from cold
  - Winding and inserting a bobbin
  - Sewing a 6-in straight seam at consistent speed
  - Sewing through a 4-layer canvas stack (simulates a bound corner)
  - Backstitching at start and end
  - Changing the needle to a 110/18
  - Identifying when to stop and ask for help (skipped stitches, thread breakage, jam)
- **Sign-off** is recorded in your member profile within 24 hours.

### Step 3: Independent reservation
- After sign-off, the walking-foot appears in your bookable equipment list
- Reserve 2-hour blocks via the Maker Nexus member portal
- Walk-up use is allowed when a block is unbooked, but during peak hours you'll want a reservation
- A textile steward is on duty during standard textile-area hours; not required to be present but available for help

## Realistic timeline for this project
- **Week 1, Tuesday eve:** orientation class
- **Week 1, Saturday:** stop in for sign-off (~30 min) + first project session
- **Week 2:** complete sessions 2 and 3

If the orientation is fully booked, ask to be added to the waitlist — cancellations are common, and stewards can sometimes run 1-on-1 sessions for members with a specific project deadline.

## Tips for passing sign-off on the first try
1. **Practice threading on the orientation-class machine before leaving the class.** Industrial threading paths have 4-6 more checkpoints than domestics; muscle memory matters.
2. **Bring your own needles in the size you plan to use.** Maker Nexus stocks general sizes but stewards appreciate when you've planned ahead.
3. **Don't fight the speed.** Industrials are touchier than domestics on the pedal. Keep your foot light.
4. **Know the difference between a thread-tension issue and a needle-strike issue.** Stewards ask diagnostic questions during sign-off, not just "did you sew a straight line."

## What if you can't get certified in time?
The domestic machines at Maker Nexus include a couple of Janome HD-3000 or HD-9 models that are rated for heavy fabrics. They can sew this project, but you'll want to:
- Use a **denim/jeans 110/18 needle** in the domestic
- Hand-walk the wheel through any 4+ layer intersection
- Use a Teflon foot or a roller foot to keep the wax from sticking to the presser
- Sew slower; expect occasional skipped stitches

A determined builder can complete this bag on a domestic. The walking-foot just makes it ~2x faster and noticeably more consistent at the stress points.
