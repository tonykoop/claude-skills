## What to check next

1. **R02 for rebound (next ~2 weeks).** Freshly-set branches sometimes lift 5–10° in the first fortnight after wire comes off, especially with strong apical pull on a ficus in active growth. Photograph from a fixed angle now so you have a baseline. If it rebounds noticeably, you can re-wire — but go to a slightly lower angle than the final goal so the spring-back lands you where you want.
2. **The impression line itself.** Inspect weekly for the next month. Flag any of: sap weep along the spiral, dark sunken tissue, or a raised welt forming on either side of the line. Absence of those = cosmetic only.
3. **New shoots on R02 and just above it.** Wire removal often coincides with a flush; on a ficus at 27°C in full morning sun you may see push within days. Decide early whether you want those shoots (taper, ramification) or whether they're competing with the line you just set.
4. **General aftercare for ~7 days.** Normal moisture checks, no fertilizer bump, no other structural work on R02. The branch isn't injured but the cambium has been compressed along the wire path and is busy normalizing.

## Will the wire mark scar permanently or fade?

Almost certainly fade — and on this species, fairly quickly.

What you described is a **wire impression**, not **bite-in**:
- *Impression* = the wire pressed into the bark surface but the branch did not grow around it. Bark is intact, no callus ridge, no sap. This is what you've got.
- *Bite-in* = the branch swelled and the bark grew up over the wire, leaving a permanent spiral scar or ridge after removal. That's the one that scars.

On a healthy *Ficus benjamina* in warm-season growth, surface impressions like yours typically smooth out as the branch continues to thicken. The bark cells expand outward and the spiral groove flattens — usually noticeably better within 4–8 weeks, mostly invisible by the end of the growth season, especially on younger smoother bark. Older, more corky sections of bark fade slower but still go. The fact that you caught it before bite-in is the whole reason it'll disappear.

Two things that would change that answer:
- If you spot a **callus ridge** forming on either side of the spiral over the next couple of weeks, that's the start of bite-in remodeling and the line will stay visible longer (still usually not permanent on ficus, but slower — months not weeks).
- If the impression is on a section already heavily lignified and corky, expect a faint trace to linger into next year even though it's healing fine.

Net: low risk, good catch on the timing, no action needed on the mark itself. Just keep the weekly photo discipline on R02 for a month and you'll see it fade in the timeline.
