# Auld Lang Syne

## Provenance

Words by Robert Burns, 1788. Melody traditional Scottish, older than
the lyrics. Both have been in the public domain for well over two
centuries — pre-1929 by an enormous margin in the US and PD
worldwide.

## Pipe arrangement

- **Range:** low A (A4) to high A (A5). All nine chanter notes used.
- **Form:** through-composed 16 bars (two verses' worth of melody
  with a slight cadential change at the end).
- **Tempo:** ♩ = 80. New Year's massed-band settings sometimes go
  faster; memorial settings slower.

## Practice tips

1. **The leap to high A.** Bars 6 and 14 leap from F# up to high A,
   sit on it for a beat, then descend G–F#. The leap is the test —
   build it from a long-tone exercise on F# → A, then add the
   doubling on the A, then layer in the descent.
2. **Verse parallelism.** Bars 9–16 are a near-repeat of 1–8. Use
   that to your advantage — get the first verse rock-solid, the
   second verse will follow.
3. **No rests, ever.** The pause between verses (`{GeG}e6 e2`) is
   six beats of held high E, then two beats of more high E, *not*
   silence. The chanter is always sounding.
4. **Memorial vs. New Year.** Same tune, different feel. For
   memorials, slow to ♩ = 60 and lean into the long notes. For
   New Year, brisk to ♩ = 96 and let the doublings drive the swing.

## Range check

All nine chanter notes appear: G, A, B, c (=C#), d, e, f (=F#), g, a.
The G is implicit in the doubling clusters; if your engraver renders
graces as small notes you'll see it on the page, otherwise it's only
in the ornament tail.
