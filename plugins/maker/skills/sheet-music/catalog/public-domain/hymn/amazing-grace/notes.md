# Amazing Grace

John Newton wrote the lyrics in 1779. The melody most people know —
"New Britain" — is older still and was first paired with Newton's
text in William Walker's *Southern Harmony* (1835). Both lyrics and
melody are public domain in the US.

## Provenance

- **Lyrics:** John Newton, 1779 — public domain.
- **Melody ("New Britain"):** trad. American shape-note, paired with
  Newton's lyrics in Walker's 1835 *Southern Harmony* — public domain.
- **This canonical ABC arrangement:** Tony Koop / Heifer Zephyr, 2026,
  MIT-licensed.

## Why it's a perfect first hymn for these instruments

The melody is **pentatonic**. That makes it a natural fit for any
6-hole NAF, drone flute, kena, fujara, or pentatonic-tuned tongue
drum without transposition or substitution. The conjunct motion and
slow tempo make it forgiving for beginners.

## Form and notable features

Four phrases of three measures each in 3/4. Every phrase ends on the
tonic G or its octave equivalents. The high D in phrase 3 is the
emotional peak — give it room.

## Per-instrument transposition guide

| Instrument | Target key | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| NAF (G-tuned) | G minor pentatonic | Uses the canonical key; almost magical fit. |
| NAF (A-tuned) | A minor pentatonic | Transpose up a step. |
| Drone flute (G-anchored) | G | Canonical; drone reinforces the tonic. |
| Kena (G minor pentatonic) | G | Canonical fit. |
| Fujara | G | Works — overtone series accommodates the pentatonic. |
| Acoustic violin | G major | Open D and G strings reinforce the tonality. |
| Floor harp | G major | One lever (F→F#) needed for the leading-tone-free pentatonic; or play in C/G modal. |

## Practice tip

This tune lives or dies on **breath control through the long notes**.
Don't truncate the dotted halves — they're the soul of the hymn. If
you run out of air, take a sneaky breath after the third beat of
those bars (write the breath mark in once you find your spot).
