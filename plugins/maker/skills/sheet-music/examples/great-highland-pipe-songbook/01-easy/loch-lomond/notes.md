# Loch Lomond (The Bonnie Banks o' Loch Lomond)

## Provenance

The lyrics first appeared in print in *Vocal Melodies of Scotland*
(1841). The melody is older — a traditional Scottish air — and is
firmly in the public domain in the US (pre-1929 by more than 80
years). Both the lyrics and the melody as printed here are PD.

## Pipe arrangement

- **Range:** low A (A4) to high A (A5). All nine chanter notes used.
- **Form:** AB, each part repeated. Slow air, ♩ = 68.
- **Adaptation:** the original tune in G major dips to a low D, which
  is below the GHP chanter. This setting raises those low notes by an
  octave (or rewrites the contour to stay on the tonic A) so the
  tune fits the chanter without rests or octave breaks. The melodic
  shape — anacrusis-rise, sit on the fifth, descend to home — is
  preserved.

## Slow-air phrasing

Loch Lomond is a tune you'd sing slowly. On pipes, you don't have
breath spans — the bag handles the air — so phrase shape is carried by
**dynamic shading inside the doubling cluster**: a slightly heavier
grace note feels like an accent, a lighter one feels like a lift.
There's no notation for that in ABC; it's a feel thing the piper
adjusts in performance.

## Practice tips

1. **Sing the original first.** Hum the melody at speed, then play
   the principal notes only with the same shape. Add doublings after
   the contour is steady.
2. **The high-A climax.** Bar 11 (`{gag}a2 g2 f2 e2`) is the
   emotional peak. Lean into the high A, then feel the descent
   relax through the chanter.
3. **Held cadence.** The final low A under one continuous bag of air
   is the test. Eight beats. Steady tone, no waver, no early lift.
4. **Sounding A note.** Remember the chanter's "A" sounds closer to
   B♭ on most modern pipes — don't try to play along with a piano
   at concert pitch unless the pianist transposes.

## Range check

All nine chanter notes appear in this arrangement: G, A, B, c (=C#),
d, e, f (=F#), g, a. The B is implicit in the cadence's `{GBG}A`
grace cluster — it appears as a grace note rather than a principal
pitch.
