# Tier 1 - 16" Hexagonal Walnut Lazy Susan
**Shop-ready plan - home garage tools only**

Tools assumed: bandsaw, drill press, hand tools, orbital sander, router, clamps.
No table saw, no jointer/planer, no CNC, no laser. Plan is built around that.

---

## 1. Key dimensions (Tier 1)

A regular hexagon has six equal sides; the **point-to-point** distance equals **2 x side length**.

| Measurement | Value |
|---|---|
| Point-to-point (long diagonal) | **16"** |
| Side length | **8"** |
| Flat-to-flat (short diagonal) | **13.856"** (13-7/8" is fine) |
| Apothem (center to midpoint of edge) | **6.928"** |
| Finished thickness | **3/4"** (one board of 4/4 walnut, surfaced) |
| Rough blank you need | **at least 14" x 16-1/2"** |

For reference, the other tiers will be 14" and 12" point-to-point - same math, just scaled.

---

## 2. Stock & cut list

Walnut, 4/4 (nominal 3/4" surfaced), straight-grained, S2S if you can buy it that way (saves you from needing a jointer).

You almost certainly cannot find a single walnut board 14" wide. Plan on a **glue-up of 2 or 3 narrower boards** edge-jointed into a panel.

**Option A - two boards (cleanest look):**
- 2 x walnut, ~7-1/4" wide x 17" long, 3/4" thick

**Option B - three boards (easier to source):**
- 3 x walnut, ~5" wide x 17" long, 3/4" thick

Add ~10% for waste / a screw-up board. Buy ~5 board feet for Tier 1.

**Hardware (for the whole project, but list it now so you order once):**
- 1 x 6" lazy susan bearing, 500-lb rated (Rockler / Lee Valley / Amazon) - goes under Tier 1
- #8 x 5/8" pan-head screws (8 ct) for the bearing
- Wood glue (Titebond II or III)
- Finish: Tried-and-true Danish oil, or Osmo Polyx, or a wipe-on poly. Walnut loves oil.

## 3. Step-by-step build - Tier 1 only

### Step 1 - Mill & glue up the panel
You don't have a jointer, so you have two realistic paths to a flat glue joint:

**Path 1 (recommended): buy S2S/S4S walnut.** The mill already gave you parallel faces and one straight edge. Rip the second edge close on the bandsaw, then clean up with a hand plane or a router-and-straightedge trick (clamp a long straightedge to the board, run a flush-trim or pattern bit along it).

**Path 2: router jointing jig.** Clamp a known-straight piece of MDF or plywood to the walnut as a fence, run a top-bearing flush-trim bit along the edge. That gives you a glue-ready edge.

Glue up the panel:
- Dry-fit first, alternate growth-ring direction (smile / frown / smile) to fight cupping.
- Glue, clamp every 8-10 inches, alternate clamps top/bottom.
- Wipe squeeze-out with a damp rag while wet.
- **Let it cure overnight** before stressing it.

After cure: scrape off dried glue, hit both faces with the orbital sander at 80 -> 120 grit just to flatten and clean.

Final panel target: ~14" x 17", 3/4" thick, flat, glue lines invisible.

### Step 2 - Lay out the hexagon

This is the part most people overcomplicate. The trick: **a regular hexagon's side length equals its circumscribed-circle radius.** So if you can draw an 8"-radius circle, you can step the six points off with a compass at the same 8" setting.

1. Find the center of your panel (diagonals from corner to corner). Mark it.
2. With a compass set to **8.000"**, scribe a circle. This circle passes through all six points of the finished hexagon.
3. Without changing the compass, place the point anywhere on the circle and step around - you'll land back at the start in exactly six steps. Mark each landing.
4. Connect the six marks with a straightedge in pencil. That is your cut line.
5. Double-check: opposite points should measure 16.000" apart, opposite flats 13-7/8".

If you don't trust the compass walk, draw a horizontal line through center, mark points 8" left and 8" right (those are two opposite corners), then use a protractor to mark 60-degree rays from center at the other four corner positions.

### Step 3 - Rough-cut on the bandsaw

- Set the bandsaw fence aside; this is a freehand cut.
- Cut **outside the line by ~1/16"**. Don't try to nail the line on the bandsaw - leave material for cleanup.
- Cut each of the six edges as a straight line. Take it slow at the points; pivot the workpiece, don't twist the blade.
- A 1/4" or 3/8" blade tracks straight cuts well at this thickness.

### Step 4 - Clean the edges to the line

You have two good options with your tools:

**Option A - straightedge-and-router (most accurate):**
- Clamp a straight piece of 3/4" plywood or MDF along your pencil line, offset by the distance from your router base edge to the bit edge.
- Use a straight bit (1/2" preferred) and rout each of the six edges down to the line.
- Or simpler: clamp a straightedge **on** the line and use a top-bearing flush-trim bit, riding the bearing on the straightedge. Six setups, one edge each.

**Option B - hand tools:**
- Clamp a square block as a fence flush with the layout line and use a low-angle block plane or a sharp chisel to pare down to it. Slower, but no setup risk on six different edges.

After this step the hexagon is at final size. Verify: all six sides should be 8.000" +/- 1/32", opposite-point measurement 16.000".

### Step 5 - Round-over the top edge

- Chuck a **1/4" roundover bit** in the router (1/8" if you want a more delicate look).
- Rout the **top edge only** of the hexagon, all the way around.
- Leave the bottom edge square - it'll sit cleaner against the bearing and the tier above won't lip over.

Take light passes, especially across end-grain at the points. End-grain on walnut tears out if you're greedy.

### Step 6 - Sand

- Orbital sander: **80 -> 120 -> 150 -> 180**, then hand-sand with 220 along the grain to kill swirl marks.
- Pay extra attention to the glue lines - any squeeze-out left will show as a light streak under finish.
- Break the bottom edge with a 220 sanding block (just a whisper, not a roundover).

### Step 7 - Mount the lazy susan bearing

The 6" square bearing goes **centered, on the bottom face** of Tier 1.

1. Find center on the bottom (same diagonal trick). Mark.
2. Trace the bearing's outline so you can see where the screw holes will land.
3. Pre-drill the four screw holes through the **inner ring** of the bearing into the walnut - drill press, 7/64" bit, **stop ~1/2" deep** (you have 3/4" of stock; do not blow through).
4. Screw the bearing down with #8 x 5/8" pan-heads.
5. The outer ring of the bearing will get screwed to whatever Tier 1 sits on (the table, or a base disc - that's a later decision).

Tip: most square lazy-susan bearings have an access hole in the top plate that lines up with one of the bottom-plate screw holes when rotated 90 degrees. That's how you reach the bottom screws after the top is mounted. Read the bearing's instructions before drilling - every brand offsets the holes differently.

### Step 8 - Finish

Walnut + oil = the easiest win in woodworking.

- Wipe on a generous coat of Danish oil or Osmo Polyx with a lint-free rag.
- Let it soak 15 min, wipe off **all** excess (failure to wipe = sticky disaster).
- Cure 24 hr.
- Light 320 scuff, second coat. Two coats is plenty for a piece that holds salt shakers.
- Dispose of oily rags in water or spread them flat outside to dry - balled-up oily rags genuinely catch fire.

## 4. Sanity checks before you cut

- [ ] Panel is flat across its width (rock test on a known-flat surface)
- [ ] Compass actually set to 8.000" - verify against a steel rule, don't trust the compass scale
- [ ] Six points step around and close cleanly (no gap on the sixth step)
- [ ] Opposite-point measurement reads 16.000" both ways across center
- [ ] Bandsaw blade is square to the table (5-cut or square test before cutting hex)
- [ ] Router bit is sharp; you only have one shot at the edges

---

## 5. Common ways this goes wrong (and the fixes)

| Mistake | Why it happens | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Hexagon comes out lopsided | Compass slipped between steps | Re-mark all six from a single circle, don't walk the compass - use protractor at 60 deg |
| Edge tear-out at the points | Routed end-grain too aggressively, or wrong feed direction | Take 1/16" passes, climb-cut the last 1/4" into each point |
| Glue line visible after finish | Squeeze-out wasn't fully scraped/sanded off | Wash the dry panel with mineral spirits before sanding - glue shows up dark |
| Hexagon won't sit flat | Panel cupped during glue-up | Alternate ring direction next time; for now, sand the high side or live with it (gravity helps once loaded) |
| Bearing screws blow through the top | Drilled too deep | Use a depth collar / piece of tape on the bit at 1/2" |

---

## 6. What to walk into the garage with

A pencil, a steel rule, a compass that opens to 8", a sharp 1/4" roundover bit, a sharp straight or flush-trim bit, and 5 board feet of 4/4 walnut. That's Tier 1.

Tiers 2 and 3 are the same process at smaller radii (compass set to 7" and 6" respectively). Once Tier 1 goes well, the others are repetition.
