# Scotland the Brave

## Provenance

The melody known today as *Scotland the Brave* is a traditional
Scottish march of uncertain origin. Versions of the tune circulated
in the late 19th and early 20th centuries; it became a fixture of
pipe-band repertoire well before 1929 and the melody is in the public
domain in the United States.

The familiar **lyrics** ("Hark! when the night is falling…") were
written by Cliff Hanley in 1951 and remain under copyright in the US,
the UK, and most other jurisdictions. **They are not transcribed
here.** This file contains the melody only.

## Pipe arrangement

- **Range:** low A (A4) to high A (A5). All nine chanter notes used.
- **Form:** AABA. A part sits around the lower half of the chanter
  (low A through the fifth, E); B part rides the upper half (high E
  through high A).
- **Tempo:** ♩ = 90, a moderate march. Many pipe bands play it
  brisker (♩ = 100–112); 90 is a comfortable beginner pace.

Beginners often start by playing the principal notes only and adding
doublings phrase by phrase. The *embellishment is the rhythm* on
pipes — but the principal notes alone are still the melody.

## Practice tips

1. **Long-tone the cadence first.** Bar 8 (`{GBG}A8`) is a held low
   A under one continuous breath of the bag. Practice that pitch
   steady before adding the doubling.
2. **A part hand position.** The A part stays in the lower half of
   the chanter — top hand barely lifts off. Build muscle memory for
   the bottom-hand walk c → e → c → A.
3. **B part octave.** The B part jumps to high A on the first beat
   of every other bar. Aim for the high A first (no wobble, no flat),
   then work the doublings around it.
4. **No rests, ever.** If you need to "stop and breathe" while
   learning, lift off the bag completely (silent practice). Don't
   write a rest into the part.
